Voice traits

Five qualities. Hold them in tension — warm and credible, plain and rigorous.

Warm & human

Speak to one person. Use "you". Sound like a real colleague, never a policy document.

Clear & direct

Lead with the point. Short sentences. Plain words over impressive ones.

Credible

Rigorous and accurate. Warmth never means dumbing the substance down.

Encouraging

Assume capability. Guide, don't lecture. Make the next step feel achievable.

Proudly African

Local context and examples, globally relevant insight. Our point of view.

Tone holds steady

We chose one consistent tone — don't swing formal for theory and chatty for activities.

i
One register, calibrated

Stay warm and plain throughout. The energy can lift slightly for prompts and encouragement, but the voice never changes character. No corporate stiffness in the lectures; no over-familiar slang in the activities.

Plain language mechanics

Writing learners can act on.

Plain language is an accessibility feature, not a downgrade. These rules make content faster to read, easier to translate into action, and consistent across every course.

Core writing rules

  • ✓
    One idea per paragraph; 2–4 sentences. Break up anything longer. White space is a feature.
  • ✓
    Active voice. "Review the case study" — not "the case study should be reviewed".
  • ✓
    Front-load the point. Say what matters in the first sentence, then explain.
  • ✓
    Plain words. "use" not "utilise", "help" not "facilitate", "about" not "with regard to".
  • ✓
    Define terms once, then stay consistent. Don't call it a "module" here and a "unit" there.
  • ✓
    Capitalise by role, not by habit. Title Case for course, module and page titles; sentence case for in-page headings and body. See the convention below.

Use the levels in order — not by looks

Choose Title, Header or Subheader for what it is in the structure, never for how it looks: Title → Header → Subheader → Paragraph. Don't skip a level (for example, Title straight to Subheader). Screen-reader and keyboard users navigate by this structure.

Say “Title Case” and “sentence case” — avoid “Capital Case”, which gets misread as ALL CAPS. “GIBS” is always all caps; use bold for emphasis, never ALL CAPS.

Numbers & currency

Based on APA-style number usage, adapted for South African / British formatting. Numerals scan faster in instructions, rubrics and LMS navigation — so learning-design values lean numeric.

★
The rule

Spell out one to nine in general prose; use numerals for 10 and above. Always use numerals for measurements, marks, percentages, dates, times, ages, numbered steps, module/topic numbers and currency. For rand, put the symbol before the number and use a space as the thousands separator: R100 000, not R100,000.

Bullets & numbered lists

Lists clarify structure — they never decorate. The choice between bullets and numbers is a content decision, not a visual one.

1
Use a numbered list when…

The items need to be followed in order, completed as steps, referenced later, mapped to an assessment, or counted. The number is meaningful.

•
Use a bulleted list when…

The items are related but the order does not matter. You could swap them around without changing the meaning.

Bullets vs numbers by course element.

Course outcomes — CO format

Number with a CO label so outcomes can be referenced in assessments, rubrics and quality reviews.

CO1. Explain the role of financial information in decision-making.
CO2. Interpret basic financial statements.
CO3. Apply financial ratios to evaluate performance.

Topic outcomes — TO format

Number topic outcomes with a TO label so they map back to course outcomes and forward to lesson activities.

TO1. Describe the purpose of an income statement.
TO2. Identify the sections of a balance sheet.
TO3. Explain how cash flow affects business decisions.

Never create fake lists

Do not type dashes, asterisks or numbers on separate lines to simulate a list. Use the built-in list tool in Blackboard, Word, Rise, Storyline or HTML. Properly marked-up lists are announced and navigated by screen readers; visually formatted symbol lists are not.

Keep list items parallel

If one item starts with a verb, all items start with a verb. If one item is a full sentence, all are full sentences. If a list exceeds seven items, consider grouping under subheadings or splitting the content into smaller sections.

The AI-tells we cut

Generated drafts lean on these. Find and replace them before anything ships.

✕Cut these

delve, unlock, elevate, leverage, harness, navigate the landscape, "in today's fast-paced world", "it's important to note that", "more than ever", "the world of…", "let's dive in", endless em-dashes, three-item lists of everything.

✓Write instead

Say the concrete thing. "Use" not "leverage". "Look at" not "delve into". Open with the actual point, not a throat-clearing preamble. Vary your sentence rhythm.

Why no icons?

Icon fonts and emoji don't render the same on every device, and most add no meaning for screen-reader users. Accordion — for layered or optional detail
When should I use an accordion?+

For supplementary detail, FAQs, or optional deep-dives — content a learner can choose to expand. Keep essential, sequential learning visible by default.

How much goes in one panel?+

A short paragraph or a tight list. If a panel runs long, it's probably its own section.

Is it accessible?+

Built on native <details>/<summary>, so it's keyboard- and screen-reader-friendly out of the box. Prefer it over custom JavaScript toggles.

Tabs — for parallel, comparable content
The concept explained plainly, in one short paragraph the learner can grasp on first read.
A concrete, ideally South African or African, example that makes the concept real.
A prompt that asks the learner to apply the concept to their own organisation.
!
Interaction rule

An interactive must add value, not just movement. If the same content reads fine as plain text, use plain text. Never put assessed or required content behind an optional interaction.

UDL + WCAG 2.1 AA

Accessible by default.

Accessibility isn't a final check — it's how we design from the first draft. Universal Design for Learning tells us to offer more than one way in; WCAG gives us the measurable baseline.

The three UDL principles

Build choice in. Different learners access, engage with, and demonstrate learning differently.

PRINCIPLE 01

Multiple means of representation

Offer content in more than one form — text, visual, audio, video — so the "what" of learning is reachable for everyone.

PRINCIPLE 02

Multiple means of engagement

Give learners reasons to care and ways to connect — relevant examples, choice, and the "why" of learning.

PRINCIPLE 03

Multiple means of action & expression

Let learners show what they know in more than one way — the "how" of learning.

A GIBS standard

Every course has an AI study assistant.

An embedded AI assistant is now part of the standard course build — a always-available study companion that helps learners check understanding, find content, and stay unstuck. It must feel like GIBS: warm, credible, and genuinely helpful.

What it is — and isn't

✓It should

Answer questions about the course content. Explain a concept another way. Point to the right module. Offer practice questions. Encourage a stuck learner.

✕It shouldn't

Do assessed work for the learner. Invent facts beyond the course. Replace faculty contact. Make decisions a human should own.

Working tools, not just rules

The toolkit.

Two helpers that put this guide to work. Both run entirely in your browser — nothing is sent anywhere, so they work offline and on any host.

UK / SA spelling troubleshooter

Paste a word, sentence, or paragraph. It flags US spellings and suggests the GIBS UK/SA form — and quietly points out a few jargon and AI-tells while it's at it.

Check my spelling

UK / SA English

A rules-and-dictionary check for the common US→UK/SA differences (‑ize→‑ise, color→colour, center→centre, program→programme, and more). It won't catch every word — treat it as a fast first pass, not a replacement for proofreading.

GIBS approved words

Some words are correct in more than one form — "self-reflection" and "self reflection" are both valid UK/SA spelling. To keep courses consistent, GIBS picks one. Add the preferred forms here and the spelling troubleshooter will flag the others and suggest the GIBS form.
